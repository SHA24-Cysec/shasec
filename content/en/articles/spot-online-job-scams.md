Fake job postings thrive because they target people who need income. The common pattern is easy work, high pay, low requirements. The real goal is not hiring but harvesting personal data, extorting fees, or turning you into a conduit for illicit funds. If an offer sounds too good, check first before sharing anything.

## Why online job scams are common
Remote work is real, so legitimate and fake online postings look similar. Scammers copy the name of a known company, use its logo, and reach out through trusted platforms. What sets them apart is the detail: unusual requests early in the hiring process. Those details are what you need to check.

## Practical steps to spot a fake posting
Check from the listing to the documents. Do not skip any before sending sensitive data.

### 1. Judge whether the listing makes sense
Very high pay for easy tasks such as "liking videos" or "typing data" is a danger sign. A legitimate listing explains the tasks, qualifications, and a reasonable salary range. Compare against industry pay for a similar role.

### 2. Verify the recruiter and company
Look up the company on its official site and professional networks. Check whether the recruiter's email uses the company domain rather than a free personal address. Contact the company's HR through an official channel to confirm the opening really exists.

### 3. Watch for fee requests
A job that asks for a registration, training, "kit", or deposit fee before you start is almost always a scam. Legitimate companies pay you, not the other way around. Any transfer of money during hiring is a red flag.

### 4. Hold back sensitive documents early
Do not send a photo of your ID, passport, face, or bank account number when you are only applying. Documents like those belong only after you are sure the offer is official and the contract is clear. Scammers use this data for loans or fake accounts.

### 5. Avoid becoming a funds conduit
If your task is to receive money and forward it to someone else, or to help "cash out" funds, you may unknowingly become a money mule. That is money laundering and carries legal risk. A legitimate task never uses your account as a bridge.

## Example: a "reviewer" asked for a deposit
Someone applies for a "product rating" role with high daily pay. After a few small tasks, they are paid a little to build trust. Then a "VIP task" appears that requires a deposit before they can work and withdraw larger earnings. The deposit never comes back. The small early payment is classic bait; the deposit request is the trap.

## If you already shared data or paid
Record all communication, account numbers, and the names involved. If an ID or personal data has been sent, monitor your credit and bank accounts. Report to the police and to the platform where the job was posted. If you already paid, ask your bank to block the transaction and report it at once.

## Common mistakes to avoid
- **Trusting high pay for easy work.** The market does not pay well for tasks anyone can do.
- **Sending an ID when applying.** Identity documents are too valuable for the early stage.
- **Accepting a fund-transfer role.** Acting as a conduit for someone else's money is legally risky.

## Frequently asked questions
### How do I confirm a listing is real?
Check trusted job portals and the company's official profile. Contact HR through a contact you find yourself, not from a recruiter's chat.

### Is it okay to send a CV?
Yes, since a CV holds your work history. What to hold back is identity documents and bank details until an offer is firm.

### What signals a money mule scheme?
You are asked to receive a transfer and forward it to another account, or to cash out funds on someone's behalf. Decline and report it.
