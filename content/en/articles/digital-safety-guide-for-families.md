Family digital safety is not about banning technology but about setting simple rules everyone understands. Children, parents, and other members face different risks: kids hit by game-prize scams, older relatives hit by banking phishing. The goal is the same: each person knows when to stop and ask before doing something harmful.

## Why a family needs shared rules
Family incidents rarely stem from one person's carelessness. Often one member clicks, and the whole family is affected: a game account hijacked, a bank account drained, a device hit by malware. Rules talked through together make the response faster and build a habit of reminding each other without scare tactics.

## Practical steps for family digital safety
Lay out rules from accounts to emergency contacts. Keep them simple so every age can remember.

### 1. Control children's accounts and shared devices
For a child's account, use parental controls and keep the main password yourself. Make sure game and app accounts are tied to an email you monitor. On shared devices, use separate profiles so history and permissions do not mix.

### 2. Teach three core rules
Set three short limits: do not click links from strangers, do not share OTPs or codes, and do not send photos of ID cards or documents. Children and less tech-savvy relatives follow three short rules far more easily than a long list.

### 3. Make a "ask first" rule before confusing actions
Agree that any member may stop and ask before downloading an unfamiliar app, paying for something, or filling in data. There is no punishment for asking, only help. This rule holds back the rushed decisions that often open the door to scams.

### 4. Separate work and personal devices
A parent's work device often holds company data. Do not let a child use it for games or browsing. Separate work accounts from personal accounts too, so one incident does not spread to both sides.

### 5. Prepare emergency contacts and help routes
Note bank numbers, device support, and trusted contacts somewhere every member can reach. If something happens, anyone in the family knows who to call first, without searching in panic.

## Example: a "game prize" that asked for a login
A child receives a message in a game promising a free skin if they log in through a provided link. Because of the rules "do not click strange links" and "ask first", they tell a parent instead of following along. The parent checks and finds it is an account-theft attempt. A short, understood rule saves the account without drama.

## If a family member is scammed or hit by an incident
Respond without blaming; shame makes people hide incidents. Limit the damage: change the related account passwords, sign out foreign sessions, and contact the bank if money is involved. Use the incident as shared learning so a similar pattern does not repeat.

## Common mistakes to avoid
- **Scaring instead of educating.** Fear makes members hide what happened instead of reporting it.
- **Leaving children's accounts unmonitored.** A game account without parental controls is a common scam doorway.
- **Having no emergency contact.** In panic, the family needs to know who to call first.

## Frequently asked questions
### Are parental controls enough?
They help, but they do not replace education. A child who understands the rules stays safer even away from home.
### At what age should teaching start?
As soon as a child uses a device. Adjust the depth of explanation to the age.
### What about teenagers who resist rules?
Negotiate the rules together and explain the reasons. Rules that are understood are accepted better than rules that are forced.
