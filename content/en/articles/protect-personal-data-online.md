Personal data is almost impossible to take back once it spreads. A photo of an ID card, an address, a full birth date, or a mother's maiden name can be used to open accounts, apply for loans in your name, or craft phishing that is highly convincing. The goal is not to stop using the internet, but to choose what really needs to be shared.

## Why personal data is worth protecting
Attackers combine small fragments: a birth date from social media, an address from a photographed package, an ID number from an old form. Together, the fragments are enough to impersonate you. Protection is not about hiding everything, but about holding back the pieces most often used to verify identity.

## Practical steps to protect personal data
Start with the most sensitive data. Each step closes one path to misuse.

### 1. Separate sensitive data from ordinary data
Mark the information used for verification: ID number, full birth date, mother's maiden name, full address, bank account number. These should never be shared in the open. Keep them distinct from information meant to be public.

### 2. Tighten what you share on social media
Hide your birth year, home address, and daily routes from public profiles. Do not photograph documents or packages that show an address. Set account privacy so only trusted contacts see personal details. Remember: old comments and photos can still be screenshotted.

### 3. Check app and service permissions
Open the permissions settings on your phone: location, contacts, microphone, camera, gallery. Revoke permissions an app does not need. Review the third-party services you authorized through "log in with Google/Facebook" and remove the ones you no longer use.

### 4. Close or tidy old accounts
Old, unused accounts often hold data and have weak passwords. Sign in, update or delete the sensitive data, then deactivate the account. Keep a list of accounts you have made so none are forgotten.

### 5. Verify every request for information
When a call or message asks for data, do not give it right away. End the conversation, then contact the organization through a number or website you find yourself. Legitimate services rarely rush you. Urgency is a warning sign.

## Example: an ID photo in a "prize" group
Someone is asked to photograph their ID card in a chat group so a prize claim can be processed. They send it. Weeks later, their data is used to register online loans in their name. Because some online lenders verify loosely, an ID number and a face photo are enough. Refusing to send documents to an unverified source, or verifying through the official channel, cuts the path off.

## If you suspect personal data leaked
Note what data was exposed and to whom. Change passwords for related accounts, especially those that use the leaked information as a security answer. Watch your bank and credit reports for foreign activity. If identity documents are involved, report it to the relevant authority so there is an official record.

## Common mistakes to avoid
- **Using security answers that are easy to guess.** A pet's name or an elementary school often shows up on a public profile.
- **Sending document photos to unfamiliar chats.** A photo cannot be "deleted" once it is sent.
- **Leaving old accounts open.** Unused accounts still hold data with minimal protection.

## Frequently asked questions

### What counts as the most sensitive data?
ID number, full birth date, mother's maiden name, address, and bank account number. These are most often used to verify identity.

### Is it okay to share a birth date on social media?
Hide the year if you can. Many services use the full date for password resets.

### How do I erase my online footprint?
Start with unused accounts: sign in, delete data, deactivate. For data held by third-party sites, file a deletion request under the service's terms.
